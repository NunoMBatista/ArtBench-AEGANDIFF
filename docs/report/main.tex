\begin{abstract}
We design, train, and evaluate multiple generative model families on ArtBench-10: a VAE, a DCGAN (and cGAN variant), and diffusion-based models, including fine-tuning of a pretrained Google DDPM. Models are selected via Bayesian hyperparameter sweeps on a 20\% subset and evaluated with FID/KID. Final results are reported for full-dataset training with evaluation repeated across 10 random seeds.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
We study generative modeling on ArtBench-10, a 10-class dataset of $32\times 32$ paintings \cite{artbench}. We implement and compare a VAE \cite{vae}, a DCGAN and conditional variant (cGAN) \cite{dcgan}, and diffusion-based models \cite{ddpm}, including fine-tuning a pretrained Google DDPM. We evaluate sample quality with FID \cite{fid} and KID \cite{kid} and report results across 10 random seeds.

\section{Problem Description}
The target domain is ArtBench-10 \cite{artbench}, a curated benchmark of 50,000 training and 10,000 test images across 10 artistic styles. Each image is a $32 \times 32$ RGB painting.

The objective is to learn a generative model $p_\theta(x)$ that can sample plausible paintings while maintaining fidelity and diversity. Key challenges include:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Fidelity:} Avoiding overly smooth or noisy samples.
    \item \textbf{Diversity:} Covering multiple styles without collapse.
    \item \textbf{Stability:} Handling adversarial (GAN) and iterative (diffusion) training dynamics.
\end{itemize}

\section{Methodology and Approach}

\subsection{Data Pipeline}
The data pipeline handles the ArtBench-10 dataset ($32 \times 32$ RGB images, 10 styles). Images are converted from integer pixels to floating point and normalized to the range $[-1, 1]$ via
$$x' = 2\cdot\frac{x}{255} - 1.$$
This normalization centers the data around zero and standardizes the dynamic range across models, which improves numerical conditioning for gradient-based optimization and aligns the data scale with common generative-model output parameterizations (e.g., a \texttt{tanh} output layer naturally produces values in $[-1,1]$). For diffusion models, using a symmetric, zero-centered range is also convenient because the forward noising process is defined around zero-mean Gaussian noise.

During the experimentation phase, a 20\% subset (10,000 images) was used for hyperparameter sweeps via Bayesian optimization. Once optimal parameters were identified, the final models were retrained on the full 50,000-image training set.

\subsubsection{VAE}
Our VAE implementation features a symmetric 4-layer convolutional architecture (32$\to$16$\to$8$\to$4$\to$2). Each layer employs Batch Normalization and LeakyReLU activations. We employ the reparameterization trick to allow for end-to-end backpropagation through the 256-dimensional stochastic bottleneck.

\subsubsection{DCGAN and cGAN}
The GAN architectures follow DCGAN \cite{dcgan}. The cGAN variant incorporates a style embedding (dimension 64) concatenated with the noise vector in the generator and injected as an additional channel in the discriminator.

\subsubsection{Diffusion (From Scratch)}
Our "TinyUNet" consists of two downsampling and two upsampling stages using Residual Blocks with Group Normalization and SiLU activations. Skip connections preserve high-frequency stylistic details. 

\subsubsection{Diffusion (Pretrained Google DDPM)}
The \texttt{google/ddpm-cifar10-32} model \cite{google_ddpm} was fine-tuned, which uses a larger UNet with self-attention layers.

\subsection{Implementation and Reproducibility}
Our pipeline is configuration-driven via YAML files. We utilize Weights \& Biases (W\&B) for real-time metric logging. All experiments were conducted using PyTorch, with random seeds fixed to 67 for hyperparameter comparisons.

\subsection{Theoretical Analysis of Hyperparameters}
We summarize the swept hyperparameters and their primary effects on optimization stability, capacity, and sample quality.

\subsubsection{Variational Autoencoders (VAE)}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Learning rate (\texttt{lr}):} Controls optimization step size; too large can destabilize training and degrade reconstructions, while too small slows convergence.
    \item \textbf{Latent dimension (\texttt{latent\_dim}):} Sets bottleneck capacity; larger values increase expressiveness but can weaken regularization and encourage blurry reconstructions if the decoder dominates.
    \item \textbf{Base channels (\texttt{base\_channels}):} Scales network width and capacity; increasing it typically improves fidelity at higher compute cost.
    \item \textbf{KL weight ($\beta$):} Scales the KL regularizer in the VAE objective, i.e., it weights the pressure for $q_\phi(z\mid x)$ to match the prior $p(z)$. With a reconstruction loss term (here, effectively an $L2$-style distortion term) and a KL term, increasing $\beta$ reduces the information capacity of the latent (it discourages encoding fine detail in $z$), typically improving sampling from the prior and latent smoothness but worsening reconstruction fidelity. Decreasing $\beta$ does the opposite: it behaves more like a deterministic autoencoder ($\beta \to 0$), often improving reconstructions but producing a latent distribution that deviates from the prior, which can harm unconditional sample quality when sampling $z\sim p(z)$.
    \item \textbf{Batch size (\texttt{batch\_size}):} Affects gradient noise and effective learning rate; larger batches stabilize optimization but reduce stochastic regularization.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Generative Adversarial Networks (GAN)}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Learning rate (\texttt{lr}):} One of the most sensitive GAN hyperparameters; higher values can cause divergence or oscillations in the discriminator and generator game.
    \item \textbf{Latent dimension (\texttt{latent\_dim}):} Controls the dimensionality of the generator input noise; too small can limit diversity, while too large can make mapping harder without improving quality.
    \item \textbf{Base channels (\texttt{base\_channels}):} Changes generator/discriminator capacity; imbalance can lead to either an overpowered discriminator (vanishing gradients) or an underpowered discriminator (poor signal).
    \item \textbf{Discriminator updates (\texttt{d\_updates\_per\_g}):} Sets the ratio of discriminator steps per generator step; increasing it can stabilize training when the discriminator is weak, but can also over-train the discriminator and stall generator learning.
    \item \textbf{Spectral normalization (\texttt{use\_spectral\_norm}):} Constrains discriminator Lipschitzness, often improving stability, but can reduce discriminator flexibility and slow learning depending on the regime.
    \item \textbf{Observed regime:} The best setting we found used \texttt{d\_updates\_per\_g}=1, \texttt{use\_spectral\_norm}=false, and \texttt{latent\_dim}=64. Samples remained prone to high-frequency artifacts consistent with transpose-convolution upsampling.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Conditional GAN (cGAN)}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Generator conditioning (concat in latent space):} Given a noise vector $z$ (dimension $d$) and a class label $y$, the generator embeds the label with an embedding layer $e(y)$ (dimension 64) and concatenates them $[z; e(y)]$. The concatenated vector is reshaped to $(d+64)\times 1\times 1$ and fed through the standard DCGAN transposed-convolution stack. This makes the class signal available from the first layer onward.
    \item \textbf{Discriminator conditioning (label as an image channel):} The discriminator embeds $y$ into a vector of size $32\cdot 32$, reshapes it into a $1\times 32\times 32$ map, and concatenates it with the RGB image as an extra input channel (input becomes $4\times 32\times 32$). The discriminator therefore learns to score joint pairs $(x,y)$ rather than marginal realism of $x$.
    \item \textbf{Effect on optimization:} Conditioning increases discriminator capacity (it can use the label map as a shortcut feature). If the discriminator learns class-specific cues faster than the generator can match them, gradients to the generator become less informative, reducing both diversity and conditional fidelity.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Diffusion Models}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Learning rate (\texttt{lr}):} Controls how quickly the denoiser fits the noise-prediction objective; too large can harm stability or lead to overshooting, while too small slows convergence.
    \item \textbf{Base channels (\texttt{base\_channels}):} Sets UNet width; larger models typically improve sample fidelity and style capture at increased compute.
    \item \textbf{Attention (\texttt{use\_attention}):} When enabled, the TinyUNet inserts a multi-head self-attention block at the bottleneck feature map (after the mid residual block, at the lowest spatial resolution). Concretely, it computes $Q,K,V$ via a $1\times 1$ convolution, flattens the $H\times W$ spatial grid into tokens, and applies scaled dot-product attention so each location can aggregate information from all other locations. The attention output is projected back with a $1\times 1$ convolution and added residually. This can improve global coherence (e.g., consistent structure across distant regions) at relatively low cost because the bottleneck has the smallest $H\times W$.
    \item \textbf{Training length:} For the from-scratch model, extending training to 50 epochs produced the largest quality gains.
    \item \textbf{Fine-tuning:} For Google DDPM, a higher learning rate ($5 \times 10^{-4}$) improved adaptation from CIFAR-10 priors to ArtBench.
\end{itemize}

\section{Experimental Setup}

\subsection{Hyperparameter Search}
Hyperparameter selection was performed on a \textbf{20\% training subset} (10,000 images) using \textbf{Bayesian optimization} (Weights \& Biases sweeps). Each sweep run trained a candidate configuration and evaluated sample quality using \textbf{FID} (minimize). For computationally expensive diffusion-based models, sweep runs used reduced evaluation settings (e.g., fewer generated samples and shorter training) and the best configuration was then retrained with the final evaluation settings. Our stopping criteria, though varying across models, was around the point where the best FID plateaued for a significant number of consecutive runs.

Several patterns emerge from the sweeps. Learning rate is the dominant factor for most models, with the parallel plots confirming that high learning rates uniformly degrade performance. An exception is the \textbf{Diffusion model}, where the binary \texttt{use\_attention} flag alone explains 96.4\% of variance, and the parallel coordinates plot shows a clean split, with all low-FID runs passing through \texttt{use\_attention=false}. At 32$\times$32 resolution, the additional complexity of self-attention appears to impair rather than help optimization. For the \textbf{VAE}, the KL weight \texttt{beta} is the second most important parameter, which is expected given its direct control over the reconstruction--regularization trade-off; the sweep selected a low value ($\beta=0.5$), prioritizing reconstruction fidelity. The \textbf{DCGAN} sweep found \texttt{use\_spectral\_norm} nearly irrelevant (0.2\%), suggesting that at this scale the standard adversarial objective is already stable enough. In contrast, the \textbf{cGAN} importance profile is remarkably flat across all five parameters (range 0.105-0.288), indicating no single knob governs training outcome, a sign that the model's failure (seen in the results section) is systemic rather than attributable to any specific misconfiguration. For the \textbf{Google DDPM}, the parallel plot reveals a clear trend: lower learning rates and more training epochs consistently yield lower FID, with \texttt{lr=0.01} leading to catastrophic divergence ($\text{FID}\approx500$).

\subsection{Experimental Protocol}
After each sweep, we used the hyperparameters in Table \ref{tab:hyperparams} to train the models on the 20\% subset. We then performed a \textbf{final evaluation} by training that best configuration once (seed 67) and computing FID/KID using \textbf{5,000 generated} samples versus \textbf{5,000 real} samples (KID computed over 50 random subsets of size 100), with the evaluation repeated across \textbf{10 different random seeds} for statistical robustness (we report Mean $\pm$ Standard Deviation). Finally, we retrained all model families on the \textbf{full 50k} training set using their best hyperparameters and repeated the same 10-seed evaluation.

\paragraph{Inception Score.}
The Inception Score (IS) \cite{is} measures two complementary properties of a generative model's output distribution: \emph{objectness} (each generated image should be confidently classifiable into some category, i.e., the conditional label distribution $p(y|x)$ should have low entropy) and \emph{diversity} (the marginal $p(y)$ should be spread across many categories, i.e., high entropy). Higher IS is better. IS is computed on fake images only, using a pretrained Inception V3 classifier, and is therefore independent of the real data distribution.

The IS ranking (Table \ref{tab:quantitative_results}) largely agrees with FID/KID: TinyUNet Diffusion leads ($4.00 \pm 0.16$), followed by DCGAN ($3.15 \pm 0.10$), Google DDPM ($2.89 \pm 0.08$), cGAN ($2.10 \pm 0.04$), and VAE ($1.90 \pm 0.05$). The VAE's near-floor score reflects the blurry, low-contrast outputs that the Inception classifier cannot confidently assign to any style. The cGAN scores only marginally above the VAE despite its conditioning mechanism, consistent with its per-class mode collapse producing coherent-looking but non-diverse outputs. Note that IS scores on $32\times32$ images are inherently lower than those reported on higher-resolution benchmarks (e.g., ImageNet), since the Inception classifier's softmax confidences decrease with lower-resolution inputs.

On the 20\% subset, the ranking shifts notably: DCGAN achieves the best KID ($90.9 \pm 1.2 \times 10^{-3}$) and the best FID ($121.87$), with Google DDPM close behind ($\text{FID}=122.82$). Diffusion (TinyUNet) ranks third by FID ($176.58$) despite dominating on the full dataset, suggesting that data volume is a stronger determinant of diffusion model quality than for GANs. The relative ordering of VAE and cGAN remains poor in both settings, though cGAN shows a notable relative improvement on the subset ($\text{FID}=301.88$ vs.\ $393.68$ on the full dataset), which indicates less severe mode collapse when the per-class data distribution is smaller.

\subsection{Ablations and Discussion}
Our results highlight several key trade-offs:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Unconditional vs. Conditional:} DCGAN outperformed cGAN significantly, which exhibits per-class mode collapse: the generator maps each class to a single fixed output, producing zero intra-class diversity. The collapsed outputs are themselves incoherent high-frequency noise, indicating the generator learned a degenerate per-class ``solution'' to fool the discriminator without capturing any meaningful structure. This is consistent with the monotonically diverging loss. The class-conditioning mechanism appears to destabilize the adversarial objective under the chosen capacity and training budget.
    \item \textbf{Diffusion Dominance:} On the full dataset, diffusion models achieved the best FID by a large margin, benefiting from a stable MSE loss landscape and skip connections. On the 20\% subset, however, it lost to other models, indicating they require more data to realize their generative advantage. Visually, they exhibit a wide diversity of styles, representing landscapes, figures, line drawings, and abstract pieces with convincing low-level texture throughout. 
    \item \textbf{Perceptual Quality:} While TinyUNet Diffusion achieved the lowest global FID, the fine-tuned Google DDPM produced the most coherent samples by visual inspection despite a higher FID. Additionally, despite good FID and producing samples with visible texture and good color diversity, DCGAN samples suffer from checkerboard noise, worsening its perceived quality. These findings highlight that metrics do not always align with human perception of quality.
    \item \textbf{VAE smoothness:} VAEs optimized with an $L2$-style reconstruction loss optimize for the mean: when multiple high-frequency outcomes are plausible, the decoder is rewarded for predicting an average, which tends to wash out texture and contrast. This effect belongs to the model/loss combination (not a single sweep knob), and helps explain the extremely smooth samples, that loosely capture the global brownish color palette of the dataset but entirely lack fine detail.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Inception Score as a Complementary Proxy.}
FID and KID measure the distance between the generated and real \emph{distributions} in Inception feature space: they penalize both low fidelity and low coverage, but they cannot separate the two failure modes. IS is useful precisely because it is distribution-free on the real side: it measures only what the generator produces, asking whether samples are visually coherent enough for a classifier to recognize them and diverse enough to cover many categories. For $32\times32$ images this property is especially valuable because FID/KID operate on upsampled $299\times299$ features, introducing interpolation artifacts that can distort the distance estimate; IS operates on the classifier's final softmax, which is more robust to resolution. A model that achieves low FID by generating blurry averages of the real distribution will be penalized by IS (low objectness), while a model that achieves high IS by generating a small number of very sharp but identical images will be penalized by FID/KID (low diversity/coverage). Together the three metrics triangulate sample quality, diversity, and distributional fidelity from different angles.

\paragraph{VAE Latent Space Analysis.}
To investigate the structure learned by the VAE encoder, we trained two variants and visualized their latent spaces via UMAP \cite{umap} using 5,000 encoded training images (Figure \ref{fig:umap_vae_both}). The first variant uses $\beta=1$ (standard VAE), where we expected the KL regularizer to align the aggregate posterior with an Gaussian prior; the second uses $\beta=0$, effectively turning the model into a deterministic autoencoder with no regularization pressure on the latent space. Without any KL penalty, the encoder is free to allocate latent dimensions arbitrarily, so we expected it to exploit the extra freedom to push each class into a distinct, tight cluster.

Both visualizations turned out to be relatively unstructured, with no clearly separable class clusters in either setting. We hypothesize that the core issue lies in the information bottleneck being too narrow for the complexity of ArtBench-10. A 32$\times$32 painting contains fine stylistic cues that are specific to each artistic style. Compressing this into a 256-dimensional bottleneck forces the encoder to prioritize low-frequency shape and global color, which are largely shared across styles, at the expense of the class-discriminative high-frequency details. The latent vectors therefore all look similar to UMAP regardless of the class label: the encoder has only retained the information that is common to all classes.

Crucially, this behavior cannot be attributed to posterior collapse (where the encoder ignores the input entirely and the KL term forces $q_\phi(z|x) \approx p(z)$), since the $\beta=0$ run disables the KL term entirely, yet still produces an unstructured latent space. The bottleneck capacity, rather than the regularization strength, appears to be the binding constraint.

\paragraph{cGAN Class-Wise Mode Collapse.}
To diagnose the cGAN's failure mode, we trained two configurations and visualized the discriminator's internal feature space via UMAP (Figure \ref{fig:umap_cgan_both}). Features are extracted from the penultimate layer of the discriminator, which receives both the image and its class label as input, so the representation captures what the discriminator considers realistic.

The \textbf{collapsed} configuration (\texttt{d\_updates\_per\_g}=1, mismatch weight $\lambda=0.2$) shows tightly packed fake clusters that do not spread across the real distribution: the generator has found a per-class fixed point that superficially fools the discriminator without covering the true intra-class diversity.

The \textbf{stable} configuration (\texttt{d\_updates\_per\_g}=5, $\lambda=1.0$) shows substantially higher cluster-wise variance in the fake distribution: the dashed KDE borders spread further and overlap more with the corresponding real borders, indicating the generator has been pushed to explore the full intra-class diversity rather than collapsing to a single mode. Two complementary mechanisms drove this improvement. First, increasing \texttt{d\_updates\_per\_g} to 5 gives the discriminator more signal per generator step, making it harder for the generator to find and exploit a degenerate fixed point. Second, raising the mismatch loss weight $\lambda$ to 1.0 penalizes the discriminator heavily for accepting real images paired with wrong labels, forcing it to learn genuinely class-discriminative features rather than settling for class-agnostic realism cues, and by extension forcing the generator to produce images that are class-specifically realistic rather than generically plausible.

\paragraph{Why Google DDPM can look better but score worse.}
FID and KID evaluate distributional similarity in a fixed Inception feature space rather than directly measuring perceived coherence \cite{fid}. The fine-tuned Google DDPM starts from a CIFAR-10 prior that encodes photographic structure; after fine-tuning on ArtBench, it generates structurally coherent images, but their visual character remains closer to natural photographs than to paintings. This stylistic misalignment is precisely what FID/KID penalize: the generated distribution diverges from the ArtBench painting statistics in texture and style cues, while human observers reward the structural plausibility. In addition, a model may produce high-quality samples while under-covering modes (reduced diversity), which is penalized by distribution-level metrics even when qualitative grids appear strong.

\section{Conclusion}
On ArtBench-10, diffusion-based models provided the best overall performance under our evaluation protocol. Quantitatively, the custom TinyUNet diffusion model achieved the lowest FID, while qualitatively the fine-tuned Google DDPM produced the most coherent samples.
