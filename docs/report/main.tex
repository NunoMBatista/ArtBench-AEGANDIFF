\title{ArtBench-10 Generative Modeling: VAE, DCGAN, and Diffusion}
\author{Nuno Batista \and Miguel Cabral Pinto}
\institute{University of Coimbra}
\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
We design, train, and evaluate multiple generative model families on ArtBench-10: a VAE, a DCGAN (and cGAN variant), and diffusion-based models, including fine-tuning of a pretrained Google DDPM. Models are selected via Bayesian hyperparameter sweeps on a 20\% subset and evaluated with FID/KID. Final results are reported for full-dataset training across 10 random seeds.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
We study generative modeling on ArtBench-10, a 10-class dataset of $32\times 32$ paintings \cite{artbench}. We implement and compare a VAE \cite{vae}, a DCGAN and conditional variant (cGAN) \cite{dcgan}, and diffusion-based models \cite{ddpm}, including fine-tuning a pretrained Google DDPM. We evaluate sample quality with FID and KID and report results across 10 random seeds.

\section{Problem Description}
The target domain is ArtBench-10 \cite{artbench}, a curated benchmark of 50,000 training and 10,000 test images across 10 artistic styles. Each image is a $32 \times 32$ RGB painting.

The objective is to learn a generative model $p_\theta(x)$ that can sample plausible paintings while maintaining fidelity and diversity. Key challenges include:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Fidelity:} Avoiding overly smooth or noisy samples.
    \item \textbf{Diversity:} Covering multiple styles without collapse.
    \item \textbf{Stability:} Handling adversarial (GAN) and iterative (diffusion) training dynamics.
\end{itemize}

\section{Methodology and Approach}

\subsection{Data Pipeline}
The data pipeline handles the ArtBench-10 dataset ($32 \times 32$ RGB images, 10 styles). Images are normalized to the range $[-1, 1]$. During the experimentation phase, a 20\% subset (10,000 images) was used for hyperparameter sweeps via Bayesian optimization. Once optimal parameters were identified, the final models were retrained on the full 50,000-image training set.

\subsection{Theoretical Analysis of Hyperparameters}
We summarize the most influential hyperparameters observed during sweeps.

\subsubsection{Variational Autoencoders (VAE)}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Mean-seeking:} With an $L2$ reconstruction term, the VAE tends to average out high-frequency details, producing smooth, low-contrast samples.
    \item \textbf{KL weight:} Lowering the KL coefficient to $\beta=0.5$ reduced regularization pressure and improved sample sharpness.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Generative Adversarial Networks (GAN)}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Stability/capacity:} The best setting used \texttt{d\_updates\_per\_g}=1, \texttt{use\_spectral\_norm}=false, and \texttt{latent\_dim}=64. Samples remained prone to high-frequency artifacts consistent with transpose-convolution upsampling.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Conditional GAN (cGAN)}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Conditioning:} We used a learned class embedding (dimension 64).
    \item \textbf{Optimization:} Conditioning increased training sensitivity; in practice, the discriminator could overfit the class signal and reduce useful gradients to the generator.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Diffusion Models}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Training length:} For the from-scratch model, extending training to 50 epochs produced the largest quality gains.
    \item \textbf{Fine-tuning:} For Google DDPM, a higher learning rate ($5 \times 10^{-4}$) improved adaptation from CIFAR-10 priors to ArtBench.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{VAE}
Our VAE implementation features a symmetric 4-layer convolutional architecture (32$\to$16$\to$8$\to$4$\to$2). Each layer employs Batch Normalization and LeakyReLU activations. We employ the reparameterization trick to allow for end-to-end backpropagation through the 256-dimensional stochastic bottleneck.

\subsubsection{DCGAN and cGAN}
The GAN architectures follow DCGAN \cite{dcgan}. The cGAN variant incorporates a style embedding (dimension 64) concatenated with the noise vector in the generator and injected as an additional channel in the discriminator.

\subsubsection{Diffusion (From Scratch)}
Our "TinyUNet" consists of two downsampling and two upsampling stages using Residual Blocks with Group Normalization and SiLU activations. Skip connections preserve high-frequency stylistic details. 

\subsubsection{Diffusion (Pretrained Google DDPM)}
We fine-tuned the \texttt{google/ddpm-cifar10-32} model \cite{google_ddpm}, which uses a larger UNet with self-attention layers.

\subsection{Implementation and Reproducibility}
Our pipeline is configuration-driven via YAML files. We utilize Weights \& Biases (W\&B) for real-time metric logging. All experiments were conducted using PyTorch, with random seeds fixed to 67 for hyperparameter comparisons.

\section{Experimental Setup}

\subsection{Hyperparameter Search}
Hyperparameter selection was performed on a \textbf{20\% training subset} (10,000 images) using \textbf{Bayesian optimization} (Weights \& Biases sweeps). Each sweep run trained a candidate configuration and evaluated sample quality using \textbf{FID} (minimize). For computationally expensive diffusion-based models, sweep runs used reduced evaluation settings (e.g., fewer generated samples and shorter training) and the best configuration was then retrained with the final evaluation settings.

We used model-specific search spaces and budgets, following the rationale in our experiment plan:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{VAE sweep:} $\{\texttt{lr},\ \texttt{latent\_dim},\ \texttt{base\_channels},\ \beta,\ \texttt{batch\_size}\}$, with an effective budget of roughly \textbf{50 runs} (out of 360 possible combinations), stopping when the best FID plateaued for approximately 10 consecutive runs.
    \item \textbf{DCGAN/cGAN sweep:} $\{\texttt{lr},\ \texttt{latent\_dim},\ \texttt{base\_channels},\ \texttt{d\_updates\_per\_g},\ \texttt{use\_spectral\_norm}\}$, with a budget of roughly \textbf{30 runs} (out of 108 possible combinations), stopping on diminishing returns in best FID.
    \item \textbf{Custom Diffusion (TinyUNet) sweep:} restricted to the most impactful knobs $\{\texttt{lr},\ \texttt{base\_channels},\ \texttt{use\_attention}\}$ (12 combinations), with about \textbf{20 runs} and reduced sweep settings (e.g., \texttt{eval\_num\_samples}=1000 and 20 epochs).
    \item \textbf{Google DDPM fine-tuning sweep:} $\{\texttt{lr},\ \texttt{epochs}\}$ (15 combinations), with about \textbf{20 runs} to balance adaptation vs. catastrophic forgetting.
\end{itemize}

After each sweep, we selected the configuration with the lowest FID on the 20\% subset. We then performed a \textbf{final evaluation} by training that best configuration across \textbf{10 random seeds} and computing FID/KID using \textbf{5,000 generated} samples versus \textbf{5,000 real} samples (KID computed over 50 random subsets of size 100). Finally, we retrained the best-performing model family on the \textbf{full 50k} training set using its best hyperparameters and repeated the 10-seed evaluation.

\subsection{Evaluation Protocol}
Following the project requirements, we generated 5,000 samples for each model and compared them against 5,000 real images.
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{FID:} Computed using the full sets of 5,000 generated and 5,000 real images \cite{fid}.
    \item \textbf{KID:} Computed using 50 random subsets of size 100.
    \item \textbf{Statistical Robustness:} The entire protocol was repeated across \textbf{10 different random seeds}. We report Mean $\pm$ Standard Deviation.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Training Dynamics}
Loss curves (Figure \ref{fig:loss_curves}) highlight different optimization behavior across model families:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{GAN:} Adversarial losses oscillate and do not necessarily decrease monotonically, consistent with a non-stationary minimax objective.
    \item \textbf{Diffusion:} Noise-prediction MSE decreases smoothly during training, reflecting a supervised regression-style objective \cite{ddpm}.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Ablations and Discussion}
Our results highlight several key trade-offs:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Unconditional vs. Conditional:} DCGAN significantly outperformed cGAN, suggesting that style-conditioning adds complexity that requires higher capacity or more training.
    \item \textbf{Diffusion Dominance:} Diffusion models achieved the best FID, benefiting from a stable MSE loss landscape and skip connections.
    \item \textbf{Perceptual Quality:} While TinyUNet Diffusion achieved the lowest FID ($43.03$), the \textbf{fine-tuned Google DDPM} produced the most coherent samples by visual inspection despite a higher FID ($97.49$).
    \item \textbf{GAN Artifacts:} Despite good FID, DCGAN samples suffer from checkerboard noise, highlighting that metrics do not always align with human perception of quality.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Why Google DDPM can look better but score worse.}
FID and KID evaluate distributional similarity in a fixed feature space rather than directly measuring perceived coherence \cite{fid}. The fine-tuned Google DDPM starts from a strong pretrained prior (CIFAR-10), which can preserve globally coherent structure after adaptation; however, its samples can remain partially misaligned with ArtBench feature statistics (e.g., texture and style cues), leading to higher FID/KID despite visually plausible outputs. In addition, a model may produce a subset of high-quality samples while under-covering modes (reduced diversity), which is penalized by FID/KID even when qualitative grids appear strong.

\section{Conclusion}
On ArtBench-10, diffusion-based models provided the best overall performance under our evaluation protocol. Quantitatively, the custom TinyUNet diffusion model achieved the lowest FID, while qualitatively the fine-tuned Google DDPM produced the most coherent samples.
